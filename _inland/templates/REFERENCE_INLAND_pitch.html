ABOUT

About INLAND

INLAND is a yearly publication documenting the soul of countries before they change. Each issue embeds with named, locatable custodians of living traditions — artisans, pastoralists, cooks, singers, border families — whose stories have not yet been narrated internationally. Previous issues have covered the Faroe Islands, Georgia and Italy's minor islands.
The editorial method is slow, place-based and built on trust. We spend months in pre-production research, identify specific custodians and communities, then commission photographers, writers and filmmakers to embed with them over extended field periods. The result is long-form visual storytelling grounded in named people and real places.
Intimacy with custodians, not thematic surveys.
THE RESEARCH

Kashmir — First Round

The Kashmir Valley is not a single story of conflict or tourism. It is a layered ecology of living traditions held together by named people and places — from pastoralists crossing the Pir Panjal to Dard-Shina speakers in border valleys, from 200-year-old food traditions in old Srinagar to women keeping alive wedding songs that predate partition.
Our first round of research identified 22 story leads spanning communities, borderlands, food cultures, ecology, music and performance. From these, twelve storylines have been selected for editorial development — each centred on a named community, a specific place, and a practice that can still be witnessed.

Our Angle

We focus on endangered knowledge systems, cross-border cultural continuity, and the under-documented living practices that survive despite climate pressure, policy change, and generational drift. Every storyline centres a named community, a specific place, and a practice that can still be witnessed.
Kashmir's living traditions are internationally under-narrated. What exists in media is dominated by conflict and tourism. INLAND offers something different: intimate, custodian-led storytelling that foregrounds cultural continuity over crisis.

Walking the Pir Panjal

Gujjar-Bakarwal Transhumance
The Story
Gujjar and Bakarwal pastoralists practise twice-yearly transhumance across the Pir Panjal, moving from winter bases in Rajouri and Poonch to summer dhoks at 3,500–4,500 m. The journey takes 6–8 weeks each way with kafilas of hundreds of sheep and goats. 39% of surveyed families have already abandoned migration. A 2025 study describes mobility as a social asset rooted in indigenous knowledge — reading snow, grass, springs, animal health and intergenerational teaching.
Our Proposal
INLAND embeds with a single extended family for the full upward crossing — documenting the daily decisions that make the route legible: when to move, how to read a slope, where to birth a lamb. The narrative follows the knowledge, not the landscape, making visible a system of ecological intelligence that disappears each time a family settles.
Places: Rajouri · Poonch · Pir Panjal passes · Kashmir Valley  |  Field timing: April–May or July–August
"The route itself is the archive."
Sources: J&K Tribal Affairs; Sage Journals 2025 mobility study; Tribal Research and Cultural Foundation

BORDERLANDS

A Border Language Survives

Dard-Shina Life in Gurez and Tulail
The Story
Gurez and Tulail valleys are home to ~25,000 Dard-Shins speaking Shina, a language closely linked to Astori Shina across the LoC. Accessible only May–November via Razdan Pass. The Habba-Khatoon Dramatic Club (founded 1978, led by Farid Kaloo) performs Shina-language songs. Recent preservation efforts include a keyboard app, community radio and dictionary work. Singer Yousuf Naseem connects Shina communities across Gurez, Drass, Kargil and Gilgit.
Our Proposal
INLAND follows the language through its living channels — a rehearsal with Kaloo's dramatic club, a radio broadcast in Shina, a kitchen where grandmothers speak words their grandchildren may not inherit. The narrative maps how a tongue survives: not through institutions but through song, app keyboards and valley roads that close each winter.
Places: Gurez Valley · Tulail · Dawar · Bandipora · 130 km from Srinagar  |  Field season: May–November
"The clearest linguistic continuity across a militarised border."
Sources: HKDC documentation; Free Press Kashmir 2026; Kashmir Life; Songlines (Yousuf Naseem)

The Ones Who Remained

Kashmiri Pandits Who Never Left
The Story
808 non-migrant Kashmiri Pandit families live across the Valley, the largest concentrations in old Srinagar. In Ranipora, the Ganjoo family is one of three Pandit households remaining from an original sixty; Muslim neighbours protected the Nagbal temple. The Kheer Bhawani Mela at Tulmulla draws 80,000 Pandit pilgrims annually.
Our Proposal
INLAND spends time with one family through the small rituals that define staying — a winter morning around the kangri, a joint wanwun sung at a neighbour's wedding, the quiet upkeep of a shared temple. The narrative centres the micro-ethics of remaining: not the politics of return, but what rootedness looks like when practised daily alongside regret.
Places: Ranipora (Anantnag) · Habba Kadal · Barbarshah · Ganpatyar (Srinagar) · Tulmulla (Ganderbal)
"Stubborn rootedness alongside regret."
Sources: KPSS 2009 survey (808 families); Sanjay Tickoo interviews; Ranipora community testimonies

COMMUNITIES

Giving Birth on the Route

Bakarwal Women's Migration
The Story
Women's labour — pregnancy, childbirth, carrying, camp-making, cooking, care, health decisions — is largely invisible in pastoral reporting. Migration depends on gendered labour absent from existing storytelling. Where does one give birth on a route? Who knows medicinal plants, danger signs, emergency paths?
Our Proposal
INLAND embeds with Bakarwal women during the spring crossing, centring their knowledge and agency rather than hardship alone. The team documents the daily decisions only women make — camp placement, water sourcing, plant medicine, the choreography of moving children and livestock at altitude. Can nest within the broader transhumance trip or stand alone.
Places: Migration corridors · Rajouri · Poonch · Pir Panjal  |  Can nest within Story 01 field trip
"What does it mean to be pregnant on a route?"
Sources: Guardian Nov 2025; PubMed health research; Gujjar-Bakarwal women's groups

Floating Homes Sinking

Hanji and the Vanishing Doonga World
The Story
Fewer than 750 houseboats remain across Kashmir; on the Jhelum, only ~61 are still afloat. Only four senior craftsmen with hull construction knowledge were on record in 2025. The British Museum's EMKP programme is documenting oral histories of remaining Hanji community members. On Anchar Lake, families face toxic landfill seepage.
Our Proposal
INLAND documents a single hull repair or construction alongside the last craftsmen who know how — recording the material intelligence of joints, timber selection and water-reading before it is lost. The team shifts the narrative from Dal Lake tourism to the Jhelum and Anchar communities living in precarity, where the story is urgent rather than picturesque.
Places: Dal Lake · Jhelum River · Anchar Lake · Srinagar
"Material intelligence disappearing in real time."
Sources: EMKP / British Museum; Indian Express; The Wire; Kashmir Houseboat Owners Association

NATURE & ECOLOGY

A Lake Gives Back a Taste

Wular Fisherfolk and the Return of Nadru
The Story
Lotus flowers returned to Wular Lake after ~30 years following large-scale desilting and willow removal. The return of nadru (lotus stem), central to Kashmiri cooking, reorders food memory and market livelihoods. Wular supports ~32,000 rural families and 2,914 active fishers.
Our Proposal
INLAND traces nadru from lake to kitchen — following fisherfolk harvesting at dawn, women cooking dishes their mothers described but never prepared, and the market dynamics of a returning ingredient. The narrative asks what a community remembers through taste, and how ecological restoration reshapes daily life from the plate outward.
Places: Wular Lake · Bandipora district
"The return of nadru reorders food memory."
Sources: The Guardian 2025; Wular Conservation and Management Authority; Kashmir Life; fisheries preprint

FOOD

Harissa Before Dawn

The 200-Year Kadals of Old Srinagar
The Story
Harissa is a slow-cooked mutton-rice porridge sold in 200-year-old eateries at three kadals in Old Srinagar, served only between 5–9 am during the three cold-season periods. Prepared overnight in underground earthen pots, pounded with a panje. Ghulam Mohammad runs a 200-year-old shop at Aali Kadal.
Our Proposal
INLAND arrives before dawn and stays through the morning rush — documenting the overnight preparation, the pounding rhythm, the regulars who have eaten here for decades. The team captures a food ritual that has never been properly photographed: the most intimate culinary story in Kashmir, told through the hands that make it and the cold that demands it.
Places: Aali Kadal · Fateh Kadal · Rajouri Kadal · Sarai Bal · Old Srinagar  |  Field timing: Dec–Feb
"The most intimate, least-photographed food story."
Sources: Ahmad family documentation (4th generation); Wazpur guild records; chillai-kalan food culture reporting

COMMUNITIES

From Tent to Talim

Changpa Pastoralists and the Source of Pashmina
The Story
The finest pashmina originates on the Changthang plateau, where semi-nomadic Changpa herd Changthangi goats at 3,700–4,500 m. The region produces ~35,000 kg of raw pashmina annually. Pashm is combed once yearly in June–July; families read the goats' coats to decide the right day, singing during combing.
Our Proposal
INLAND follows a single fibre from tent to talim — the combing on the plateau, the sorting in Leh, the spinning and weaving in Srinagar. The team builds a material biography that connects high-altitude pastoral life to the urban textile world, revealing how many hands and how much knowledge a single shawl carries from goat to finished garment.
Places: Changthang plateau · Nyoma/Rupshu · Leh · 3,700–4,500 m  |  Combing season: June–July
"From tent to talim, from goat to shawl."
Sources: ICAR Changthang studies; Mongabay 2026; ladakhpashmina.org; Textile Paths research

FOOD

Dawn in Pampore

Saffron Harvesting at the Edge of Viability
The Story
Saffron production fell ~67.5% over two decades. Area contracted from 5,707 ha to ~3,715 ha. ~30,000 families farm saffron; ~11,000 women do harvesting and stigma-separation. Mushtaq Mir, a 4th-generation Pampore grower, leads the fight against adulteration. The harvest window is just 14–28 days.
Our Proposal
INLAND positions the reader in a pre-dawn Pampore field during the brief autumn harvest, following a multigenerational family from plucking to stigma-separation. The team documents the 14-day window when five hundred years of cultivation knowledge compresses into a single intense season — the hands, the light, the economics of a crop at the edge of viability.
Places: Pampore · Lethpora · Samboora · Pulwama district  |  Harvest window: late Oct–mid Nov (14–28 days)
"14–28 days. That is all the harvest allows."
Sources: FAO GIAHS designation; J&K Dept of Agriculture; Goya magazine; SKUAST-K Dussu

MUSIC & PERFORMANCE

The Last Wanwun Nights

Women Keeping the Wedding Song
The Story
Wanwun is a Kashmiri wedding song form: rows of women, arms on shoulders, sway and sing responsorial couplets improvised on the spot. Both Muslim and Pandit women share variants. Threatened by DJ culture and compressed wedding schedules, the tradition survives in rural villages and diaspora preservation efforts.
Our Proposal
INLAND embeds in a single maenzraat — the pre-wedding henna night where wanwun unfolds — capturing the nocturnal scene rather than surveying the tradition. The team records the voices, the swaying rows, the improvised verses that carry blessings and teasing in equal measure. An embodied archive of language, ethics and women's sociality, documented as it happens.
Places: Rural weddings around Anantnag · Budgam · Shopian  |  Needs access to a real maenzraat
"Embodied archive of language, ethics and women's sociality."
Sources: Kashmiri wedding song ethnography; diaspora wanwun compilations; community documentation

BORDERLANDS

Turtuk

Border-Divided Balti Families
The Story
Turtuk and Tyakshi in Ladakh are exceptional for cross-border family memory. Fixed villages, Balti language, orchards, domestic architecture. Communities divided by India's 1971 advance into Pakistani-controlled Baltistan. Families remember relatives across the divide.
Our Proposal
INLAND sits with families who remember the other side — documenting the domestic details that carry memory: an orchard planted by a grandfather now across the border, a Balti lullaby shared with cousins who became foreigners overnight. The narrative frames home not as geography but as continuity, making it ideal for a later extension into Baltistan itself.
Places: Turtuk · Tyakshi · Nubra Valley · Ladakh  |  Border permits required
"What home means after borders move."
Sources: Mountain Communities research; Baltistan community records; border-permit documentation

COMMUNITIES

Chopan Shepherds

Remembering the Mountain for Other People's Herds
The Story
The Chopan are specialised transhumant shepherds who often do not own land or flocks, yet possess detailed ecological knowledge of rotational grazing and marginal pastures. A 2025 study argues their grazing is ecologically efficient, based on adaptive mobility and traditional ecological knowledge. Less internationally covered than Gujjar-Bakarwal pastoralists.
Our Proposal
INLAND walks with a Chopan shepherd through a grazing rotation — recording the decisions that make marginal pastures productive: which slope, which season, which grass to leave. The narrative reveals knowledge without ownership, showing how people who remember the mountain for other people's herds carry an ecological intelligence that policy rarely recognises.
Places: Alpine meadows across Kashmir Valley · Trajan pasture  |  Can nest within Story 01 trip
"Knowledge without ownership."
Sources: Frontiers Partnerships 2025; Greater Kashmir; highland pasture rights research

OUR SUGGESTION
Suggested Editorial Tracks
From the twelve storylines, we propose three production tracks as a starting point for a pilot project — each grouping stories that share geography, field timing and editorial logic into a single deployable trip. The remaining storylines provide a foundation for expansion once the pilot is complete.
THREE TRACKS

Production Tracks

The Highland Route
Stories 01 + 04 + 12
A multi-week field trip embedded with Gujjar-Bakarwal and Chopan pastoralists during the spring migration. Three nested angles within a single journey: the transhumance itself, women's invisible labour on the route, and the Chopan shepherds who remember the mountain for other people's herds.
Geography: Rajouri → Pir Panjal → Kashmir Valley
Season: April–May (upward migration) or July–Aug (summer dhok)
3 stories, 1 field deployment
The Textile Path
Story 08 — extends to Srinagar artisan world
A material biography following pashmina from Changpa herders on the Changthang plateau through dehairing, spinning and weaving to the finished shawl. One fibre, many hands — the story connects high-altitude pastoral life to Srinagar's textile communities in a single narrative arc.
Geography: Changthang plateau → Leh → Srinagar
Season: June–July (combing season)
Material biography format
Srinagar Seasons
Stories 07 + 09 + 10
A seasonal food-and-culture cluster anchored in Old Srinagar's pre-dawn harissa kadals, Pampore's autumn saffron harvest, and the nocturnal wanwun wedding song tradition. Three stories bound by seasonality, women's knowledge and under-photographed intimacy.
Geography: Old Srinagar → Pampore → Rural villages
Season: Oct–Nov (saffron), Dec–Feb (harissa), wedding season
3 stories, seasonal field windows
These three tracks represent a suggested starting point for a pilot project. The full set of twelve storylines offers scope for expansion across subsequent editorial cycles.

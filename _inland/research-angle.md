# The INLAND angle — a territory-agnostic research blueprint

*Read this before researching or producing **any** edition. It defines the kind of story
INLAND tells, in any country. `about-inland.md` says what INLAND is and `deliverables.md`
says what files to build; this file says **how to find and frame the stories** so a new
territory reads like INLAND.*

> **Rwanda and Kashmir are blueprints of the _angle_, not templates of the _subject_.**
> Do not transplant Rwandan or Kashmiri stories onto a new country. Use them only to
> recognise the *shape* of an INLAND story, then find that shape afresh in whatever
> territory the opportunity makes relevant.

---

## How the territory is chosen

The territory is **set by the opportunity**, not by us. An edition exists because a grant,
institute, residency, or partnership makes a specific place fundable (see
`opportunity-criteria.md`). So:

1. Take the territory (or candidate territories) from the opportunity's `territory` /
   `fitRationale` / brief.
2. If the opportunity names a region rather than a country, or leaves it open, choose the
   territory **within the funder's eligible geography** that best fits the INLAND profile
   below — richest under-documented living traditions, most fundable partnership.
3. Then run the angle below **on that territory**. Every country has its own custodians,
   its own ruptures, its own "what survived" — go and find them. Never default to Rwanda
   or Kashmir.

---

## The single question every edition asks

> **What survived — and can still be witnessed and photographed today?**

Every territory has a loud, internationally legible story (Rwanda → the 1994 genocide and
the "development miracle"; Kashmir → conflict and the Line of Control). That loud story
**crowds out the quieter country**. INLAND goes to the quieter country: what persists in
specific families, villages and practices that has *not* been absorbed by colonial
inheritance, mission/religion, the nation-building project, the market, or modernisation —
and is still alive enough to be filmed and visited in a real season.

This is **an act of listening, not a critique**. Continuity over crisis; dignity over
spectacle; the particular over the postcard.

---

## The non-negotiable quality bar (applies to every lead)

A topic is only an INLAND story if it has all three:

1. **A named protagonist or a specific custodian community** — a real person/family/group
   you could re-contact, not an abstraction ("the artisans of X" is not enough; name one).
2. **A locatable place** — a village, valley, sector, workshop, mountain, market you can
   put on a map and reach.
3. **A visual scene that exists in a real season** — a thing that *happens* (a harvest, a
   brew, a rehearsal, a migration, a night ritual) at a knowable time of year, that a
   photographer/filmmaker could embed in.

If a lead lacks any of the three, it goes to the bench (researched, held) — not into the
selected set. No abstract narratives.

## Recurring story types (where INLAND stories tend to live)

Use these as a checklist when scanning a new territory — most editions draw 8–12 leads
across several of them:

- **Endangered knowledge held by named individuals** — a craft, instrument, or oral form
  that may end with one or two living masters (Rwanda: the *inanga* zither's possible last
  master; Kashmir: Baand Pather folk theatre's seventh-generation artist).
- **Food as ritual memory, and the most visual category** — a dish/drink/grain that is
  really social infrastructure (marriage, dowry, offering, reconciliation), being squeezed
  by commercial substitutes or crop policy (Rwanda: foot-crushed banana beer *urwagwa*,
  sorghum beer *ikigage*; Kashmir: saffron on the Karewas, apple economies of Shopian).
- **Borderlands, minorities, displacement, return** — communities cut by a frontier, who
  stayed when others left, or moved off land and live its long afterlife (Rwanda: the
  Gishwati forest resettlement; Kashmir: Dard-Shina of Gurez on the LoC, the Pandits who
  stayed).
- **Ritual, faith and what went underground** — pre-modern/pre-colonial belief that was
  targeted yet adapts and persists, often semi-privately (Rwanda: *Kubandwa* spirit
  possession; healer–hospital referral for *uburozi*).
- **Music, song and oral culture as a women's or community archive** (Rwanda: *ibyivugo*
  poetry, women drummers; Kashmir: *wanwun* wedding songs).
- **Human–nature relationship under ecological/climate pressure** — pastoralism,
  transhumance, agrarian life as climate rewrites it (Kashmir: Gujjar–Bakkarwal
  migration; apple growers and hailstorms).
- **The modern showcase with a photographable underside** — development/infrastructure
  made human through one frame (Rwanda: the data centre beside recently-electrified
  villages). Always anchor to a person, or it stays on the bench.

These are prompts, not a quota. The right leads are whatever this specific territory holds.

## Constraints to honour in every territory (the framing discipline)

- **The loud story is context, never the lens.** Genocide (Rwanda), armed conflict and
  surveillance (Kashmir), and whatever dominates the next country, may sit in the
  background of a story but is **never its primary subject**. International coverage is
  already saturated there; INLAND's value is everything else.
- **No adversarial framing of the state.** Stories can hold tension and contradiction
  without casting a government as villain — especially where a ministry, embassy, or
  institute is a potential partner. *Tension, not attack.*
- **Consent and discretion for living, semi-private practice.** Sacred or semi-secret
  rituals are approached with consent, not staged as spectacle; named protagonists drawn
  from secondary reporting must be re-contacted and must agree before appearing publicly
  in a pitch.
- **Ground every claim** in named people, specific places, dates/seasons, and
  **sources/links** throughout — exactly as the Rwanda and Kashmir research does.
- **Voice:** slow, intimate, restrained, anthropological, cinematic. Never romanticising,
  never reducing a place to conflict or postcard.

## How to apply this to a new territory (the recipe)

1. Read `about-inland.md` (identity/voice/themes) and this file.
2. Establish the territory from the opportunity (see "How the territory is chosen").
3. Name the loud story for that place, then **set it aside** as background.
4. Run the **recurring story types** checklist as deep desk research: for each promising
   thread, find a named custodian, a locatable place, and a seasonal visual scene, with
   sources. Aim for ~15–20 researched leads.
5. Apply the **quality bar**; cut anything missing one of the three; keep the rest on a
   bench.
6. Select **8–12 leads** balanced across categories for the edition, each written as
   **Place · Angle · Practices · Status** (see `deliverables.md`), preserving source links.
7. Note **field-verification needs** and **open questions** honestly (what must be
   confirmed on the ground, what consent is still required).
8. Then build the asset set per `deliverables.md`, and shape the application documents to
   the specific funder.

## Worked examples (study the shape, don't copy the content)

- **Rwanda** — `editions/goethe-africa-europe-2026/deep-research-report.md` and
  `selected-story-leads.md`. Note the explicit editorial frame ("what survived?"), the
  carried constraints, and the *Place · Angle · Practices · Status* leads.
- **Kashmir** — `../Kashmir/inland-kashmir-selected.md` and the wider corpus in
  `../Kashmir/`. Same shape: named protagonists, locatable places, seasonal scenes,
  conflict kept as background.

The two could not be more different as places — and yet both are unmistakably INLAND.
That sameness-of-angle across difference-of-subject is exactly what a new edition must
reproduce.

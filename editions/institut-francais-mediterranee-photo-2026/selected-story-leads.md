# INLAND Tunisia — Selected Story Leads

*The curated subset chosen for editorial development from the deep-research corpus. Twelve
leads, balanced across INLAND's categories. Each is structured as **Place · Angle · Practices ·
Status**, preserving the research trail. For the Institut français Mediterranean photography
support, leads marked ★ form the proposed first production clusters; the signature long-form
story is marked ✦.*

---

## 01 ✦ The Palm in the Sea — the *Charfia*
**Category:** Nature / Food

- **Place:** the **Kerkennah Islands** off **Sfax** — low, palm-fringed islands worked by
  *charfia* fishing families.
- **Angle:** a fence of **date-palm fronds** planted in rows across the shallows to form a great
  V that channels fish on the **ebb tide** into capture chambers, the grounds shared by a draw
  of lots and worked to a maritime calendar. Inscribed by **UNESCO** as Intangible Cultural
  Heritage in **2020** — and now threatened by sea-level rise, plastic and youth emigration.
- **Practices:** cutting and planting the palm fronds; reading the tide and the lunar calendar;
  the dawn lift of the traps; the allotment of fishing grounds.
- **Status:** Endangered living tradition; the edition's signature recurring image — a fishing
  architecture built entirely from the palm, standing in the Mediterranean.
  *Sources: UNESCO ICH 2020 (Charfia fishing in the Kerkennah Islands); Earth Journalism
  Network; New Arab.*

## 02 ★ The Women Who Build with Earth — Sejnane Pottery
**Category:** Arts & Culture

- **Place:** **Sejnane**, in the **Mogods hills** behind **Bizerte**.
- **Angle:** women hand-build pots, animal figurines and the famous **Sejnane dolls** from wadi
  clay with **no wheel and no kiln**, firing in open ground and painting with two-tone geometric
  signs drawn from tattoo and Berber weaving — a women's craft with **protohistoric roots**,
  passed mother to daughter for some three thousand years. UNESCO-inscribed **2018**.
- **Practices:** digging and kneading the clay; hand-shaping; the open ground-firing; painting
  with oak-bark and natural pigment; roadside sale.
- **Status:** Living but pressed by market substitutes; one of the Mediterranean's oldest
  surviving ceramic traditions, entirely in women's hands.
  *Sources: UNESCO ICH 2018 (Pottery skills of the women of Sejnane); The Arab Weekly; phys.org.*

## 03 ★ The Island of Three Prayers — Djerba's Ibadis & El Ghriba
**Category:** Myth & Religion / History

- **Place:** **Djerba** — Ibadi mosques across the island; **El Ghriba** synagogue at **Erriadh
  / Hara Seghira**; the Jewish quarter of **Hara Kebira**.
- **Angle:** the only place in the Arab world where **Ibadi, Maliki, Hanafi, Christian and
  Jewish** communities have shared one small, waterless island for centuries — low fortified
  (sometimes half-underground) Ibadi mosques, *menzel* farmsteads and rain-cisterns, and at El
  Ghriba (**Africa's oldest synagogue**) the spring **Lag BaOmer** pilgrimage. UNESCO-inscribed
  **2023** as a settlement pattern of coexistence.
- **Practices:** prayer in a fortified Ibadi mosque; the *menzel* courtyard and cistern; the Lag
  BaOmer procession and candle-offerings.
- **Status:** Living; the Jewish community is small but the pilgrimage endures. Consent and
  discretion at the pilgrimage.
  *Sources: UNESCO WHC 2023 (Djerba); El Ghriba (Wikipedia); The Arab Weekly.*

## 04 ★ The Villages That Speak the Old Tongue — Chenini & Douiret
**Category:** Local communities / History

- **Place:** **Chenini** and **Douiret**, ridge villages of the **Dahar** above **Tataouine**.
- **Angle:** Amazigh hill-villages clinging to the rock around honeycombed **ghorfa** granaries
  and a white mosque, where a dwindling number of elders still speak **Tamazight** — even as
  families move down to "new" villages with roads and water, leaving the old houses to the wind.
  One of the last living Berber-speaking pockets of a country that mostly forgot it was Amazigh.
- **Practices:** the olive-and-barley year; the ghorfa granary; Tamazight spoken at home; the
  move between old and new village.
- **Status:** Endangered language and settlement; living but thinning generation by generation.
  *Sources: Carthage Magazine (Amazigh heritage); WildyNess; Discover Tunisia.*

## 05 The Clock That Runs Backward — Testour's Andalusians
**Category:** Arts & Culture / History

- **Place:** **Testour**, in the **Medjerda valley** (Béja).
- **Angle:** a town rebuilt in the **17th century** by **Moriscos** expelled from Spain, whose
  Great Mosque carries a **clock turning counter-clockwise** — read locally as the exiles'
  longing to wind time back toward Andalusia — and which keeps the **malouf**, the
  Arab-Andalusian music carried across the sea, alive at **Tunisia's oldest traditional-music
  festival**.
- **Practices:** malouf rehearsal and performance; the festival; the backward clock and brick
  mosque; the Andalusian thread in food and pomegranate orchards.
- **Status:** Living; a four-century-old Mediterranean exile still audible and visible.
  *Sources: The Arab Weekly; The National; Tunisian malouf (Wikipedia).*

## 06 ★ The Oasis by the Sea — Gabès
**Category:** Geopolitics / Nature

- **Place:** the **seaside oasis of Gabès** (Chenini Gabès, Jara) on the **Gulf of Gabès**.
- **Angle:** the **Mediterranean's only seaside oasis** — a three-tier garden of **date palm
  over pomegranate and henna over vegetable**, running down to the shore and watered for
  millennia — now poisoned at its edge by the state **phosphate works** that have piled
  phosphogypsum into the gulf for fifty years, with **henna and pomegranate failing** as salt
  and pollution rise.
- **Practices:** the layered oasis cultivation; **henna** growing and processing; the old
  irrigation; the gardeners' daily work beside the industrial margin.
- **Status:** Living but acutely threatened. **Tension, not indictment** — the gardeners, not
  the smokestack, are the subject.
  *Sources: Med-O-Med (Gabès oasis); Coda Story; TIMEP; SEVENSEAS.*

## 07 ★ The Spirits Carried North — Stambeli
**Category:** Myth & Religion / Music

- **Place:** **Tunis** — above all **Dar Barnou**, once a refuge for freed slaves, and **Sidi
  Saad**.
- **Angle:** the descendants of sub-Saharan people brought across the Sahara keep **Stambeli**,
  a night music of the **gambri** lute and iron **shqasheq** that calls a pantheon of
  sub-Saharan spirits and Muslim saints to **heal through trance**. Black Tunisia's living
  liturgy, surviving in only a few brotherhoods.
- **Practices:** the tuning of the gambri; incense and the colours each spirit demands; the
  chants and the trance dance; the healing ceremony.
- **Status:** Endangered; held by a handful of houses. **Consent-led; ritual, not spectacle.**
  *Sources: Stambeli (Wikipedia); Richard C. Jankowsky, Stambeli (U. Chicago Press); The Arab
  Weekly; Pan-African Music.*

## 08 The Last Matanza — Sidi Daoud
**Category:** Food / Nature

- **Place:** **Sidi Daoud**, on the tip of **Cap Bon** (near El Haouaria).
- **Angle:** fishermen set the **madrague** — a labyrinth of nets the locals call the
  **matanza** — to trap migrating Atlantic **bluefin** between **May and July**, a
  **Phoenician-old** technique that ends in a churning chamber of tuna hauled by hand. One of
  the Mediterranean's last working tuna traps, nearly stilled by quotas, industrial fleets and
  warming water.
- **Practices:** building the net-rooms; the watch for the school; the *matanza* itself; the
  haul and the shore.
- **Status:** Endangered; a violent, ancient sea-harvest at the edge of disappearing.
  *Sources: INSTM Bulletin (madrague Sidi Daoud); Mattanza (Wikipedia); harissa.com.*

## 09 White Gold — Cap Bon's Orange Blossom
**Category:** Food / Nature

- **Place:** **Nabeul** and **Hammamet**, on **Cap Bon**.
- **Angle:** each **April–May**, families pick **bitter-orange blossom** by hand before dawn,
  when the scent peaks, and distil it over slow fire in copper alembics into **zhar**
  (orange-blossom water, for sweets and grief and celebration) and the precious **neroli** oil —
  roughly a **million flowers** to a kilogram of oil. For two weeks the whole coast smells of
  one tree.
- **Practices:** the pre-dawn hand-picking; the *qattara* still over the fire; the bottling of
  zhar and neroli; the family orchard.
- **Status:** Living but seasonal and squeezed; a Mediterranean perfume-and-kitchen tradition.
  *Sources: Africanews (Nabeul orange blossom); Middle East Eye; Biolandes.*

## 10 The Gold-Threaded Loom — Mahdia Silk
**Category:** Arts & Culture

- **Place:** the sea-walled medina of **Mahdia**, on the **Sahel** coast.
- **Angle:** weavers still work tall **draw-looms** — a technology that crossed the
  Mediterranean from Iberia, the craft brought by Jewish artisans — to make narrow silk strips
  threaded with **gold and silver** for the **keswa el-kbira**, the layered wedding dress that
  signals a Sahel woman's married status. A marriage you can read in the gold of the cloth.
- **Practices:** the draw-loom and shuttle; the gold-and-silver thread; the dressing of the
  bride; the family workshop.
- **Status:** Living but ageing; pressed by imports. Best filmed around a real wedding, with the
  family's consent.
  *Sources: Carthage Magazine (Tunisian wedding); Discover Tunisia (regional crafts);
  shirahime.ch.*

## 11 The Knot and the Sweet — Kairouan
**Category:** History / Food

- **Place:** **Kairouan**, Islam's fourth holy city and its first in Africa; the UNESCO-listed
  medina.
- **Angle:** the holy city is also Tunisia's **carpet capital**, where women knot the
  **zerbia** — hundreds of thousands of knots a square metre — in workshops around the Great
  Mosque, and bake the date-and-semolina **makroudh** the city claims as its own. Both loom and
  oven are pressed by cheaper imports.
- **Practices:** dyeing the wool; the knotting frame; the *makroudh* dough, frying and honeying;
  the medina workshop.
- **Status:** Living; the holy city seen through the women's hands that keep its two crafts.
  *Sources: Discover Tunisia (weaving & carpets); Folk Culture (Kairouan carpet); WildyNess.*

## 12 ★ The Green Mountain — Aïn Draham & the Kroumirie
**Category:** Local communities / Nature

- **Place:** **Aïn Draham** and the **Kroumirie** range above **Tabarka** (Jendouba).
- **Angle:** a green, wet, oak-forested Tunisia that contradicts the postcard — where it
  **snows** in winter and the **cork-oak forest** is unlike anywhere else in the country — and
  the **Khroumir** mountain people harvest **cork** on its long bark-cycle, herding and
  gathering in the wettest microclimate in Tunisia.
- **Practices:** the summer cork-stripping (the orange-stripped trunks); herding; gathering;
  the highland village year.
- **Status:** Living; the Mediterranean Tunisia of oak and rain rather than palm and dune.
  *Sources: Carthage Magazine (Aïn Draham); Tunisi.info (Kroumirie); Take Your Backpack.*

# INLAND Tunisia — Deep-Research Report

*Prepared for the **Institut français — Mediterranean photography support** (publication +
production strands). Produced by the INLAND Producer, 2026-06-23, as a desk-research brief on
Tunisia's living traditions, following INLAND's editorial method (`_inland/research-angle.md`).
Sources are cited inline; every selected lead is held to the three-part bar — a **named
custodian or custodian community, a locatable place, and a visual scene that exists in a real
season.***

---

## TL;DR

Tunisia is internationally legible through three loud stories — the **2011 revolution** that
began the Arab Spring, the **Mediterranean migration crossings** that leave from Sfax and
Zarzis for Lampedusa, and the **beach-resort coast** of Hammamet, Sousse and Djerba. All three
are loud; all three crowd out the quieter country. INLAND's Tunisia edition asks one question
across every lead: **what survived?** What persists in specific families, islands and seasonal
practices that has been absorbed by neither the Phoenician–Roman antiquity the tour buses
visit, the French protectorate, the resort economy, nor the post-revolution turbulence — and
can still be *witnessed and photographed* today.

The image the edition takes as its emblem is the **charfia** of the Kerkennah Islands: a fence
of date-palm fronds planted in rows across the shallows to channel fish on the ebb tide, worked
to a lunar calendar — inscribed by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage in **2020**
([UNESCO ICH](https://ich.unesco.org/en/RL/charfia-fishing-in-the-kerkennah-islands-01566)).
A structure built entirely from the palm, standing in the Mediterranean, is the edition's
recurring frame. Working title: **_The Palm in the Sea — Living Traditions of Mediterranean
Tunisia_.**

Because the grant **sets the geography** (the *monde méditerranéen*) and leaves the country
open, Tunisia is chosen as the Mediterranean territory whose under-documented living traditions
are richest and most layered — a single small country that is at once Phoenician, Roman,
Amazigh (Berber), Arab, Andalusian, Jewish, Ibadi, sub-Saharan and Ottoman. The edition reads
that palimpsest through people on its shores and islands. This is not a critique of the state,
and the revolution and the migration crisis are **context, never the lens**. INLAND Issue 03
(*Isole Minori*) already proved this Mediterranean register; this edition is the next one.

The corpus below yields **17 researched leads**, of which **12 are selected** for the edition
(see `selected-story-leads.md`).

## Editorial frame

> **What the Mediterranean carried in, and what stayed.**

Tunisia's coast has been a landing-place for three thousand years — Phoenicians at Carthage,
Romans, Arabs, Andalusian Moriscos expelled from Spain, Jewish communities of great antiquity,
sub-Saharan people brought across the Sahara in bondage, Ottoman and Italian and French
administrators. Each arrival left a practice, a craft, a liturgy, a way of building or eating
or grieving. The edition documents what is still here on the shore and the islands, and still
alive enough to film.

**Constraints carried into field and pitch (honour these):**
- **The revolution and the migration crisis are context, never the lens.** International
  coverage is saturated there; they may sit in the background of a story but are never its
  primary subject.
- **No adversarial framing of the state.** Stories can hold contradiction — the seaside oasis
  of Gabès beside the state phosphate works; cheap imports against the loom — without casting
  the government as villain. Tension, not attack, especially where a ministry, the Institut
  national du patrimoine, or a French cultural partner may be involved.
- **The Mediterranean theme is the grant's hard condition** and INLAND's natural register: the
  sea as crossing, frontier, larder and memory runs through every selected lead.
- **Quality bar:** every lead needs a named protagonist or custodian community, a locatable
  place, and a visual scene that happens in a real season. No abstract narratives.
- **Consent and discretion** for living, semi-private practice (the Stambeli rite; the El
  Ghriba pilgrimage; family weddings) — approached with the community, never staged.

## Key findings

1. **The Kerkennah *charfia* is the strongest single sea-story in the country — and it is
   officially endangered.** On the low, palm-fringed Kerkennah Islands off Sfax, fishermen
   build the **charfia**: rows of date-palm fronds planted in the seabed to form a great V that
   channels fish on the ebb tide into capture chambers, the fishing grounds allotted by a draw
   of lots and worked to a maritime calendar. UNESCO inscribed **"Charfia fishing in the
   Kerkennah Islands"** on the Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage in **2020**
   ([UNESCO ICH](https://ich.unesco.org/en/RL/charfia-fishing-in-the-kerkennah-islands-01566)).
   Sea-level rise, plastic, overfishing and youth emigration now threaten it
   ([Earth Journalism Network](https://earthjournalism.net/stories/the-kerkennian-charfia-between-tradition-and-extinction);
   [New Arab](https://www.newarab.com/features/fishing-traditions-under-threat-tunisias-kerkennah-islands)).
   A family planting a fence of palm in the sea is the edition's defining image.

2. **The women of Sejnane hand-build a Mediterranean ceramics older than the wheel.** In the
   Mogods hills behind **Bizerte**, the women of **Sejnane** dig clay from wadi beds and build
   pots, animal figurines and the celebrated **Sejnane dolls** entirely by hand — no wheel, no
   kiln — firing them in open ground and painting them with two-tone geometric signs drawn from
   tattoo and Berber weaving. The methods have **protohistoric roots, passed mother to daughter
   for some three thousand years**, and every stage is performed by women, who also sell the
   work roadside. UNESCO inscribed **"Pottery skills of the women of Sejnane"** in **2018**
   ([UNESCO ICH](https://ich.unesco.org/en/RL/pottery-skills-of-the-women-of-sejnane-01406);
   [The Arab Weekly](https://thearabweekly.com/women-tunisias-sejnane-keep-pottery-tradition-alive);
   [phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2019-05-mother-daughter-tunisia-potters-ancestral.html)).

3. **Djerba is the only place in the Arab world where Ibadi, Maliki, Hanafi, Christian and
   Jewish communities have shared one small island for centuries — and UNESCO listed exactly
   that.** Ibadi Muslims built low, fortified, sometimes half-underground **mosques** across a
   dispersed island of *menzel* farmhouses and rain-cisterns; at **El Ghriba**, traditionally
   the **oldest synagogue in Africa**, Jewish pilgrims still gather each spring for the **Lag
   BaOmer** procession. In **2023** UNESCO inscribed **"Djerba: Testimony to a settlement
   pattern in an island territory,"** naming the Ibadi mosques, El Ghriba and the Jewish
   quarters of **Hara Kebira** and **Hara Seghira (Erriadh)**
   ([UNESCO WHC 1932](https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/1932/);
   [El Ghriba, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Ghriba_Synagogue);
   [The Arab Weekly](https://thearabweekly.com/tunisians-rejoice-unesco-adding-djerba-world-heritage-list)).

4. **Two Amazigh hill-villages still speak the old tongue.** On the ridges of the **Dahar**
   above **Tataouine**, **Chenini** and **Douiret** cling to the rock around honeycombed
   **ghorfa** granaries and a white mosque; a dwindling number of elders still speak
   **Tamazight** where a generation ago far more did, even as families move down to "new"
   villages with roads and water
   ([Carthage Magazine](https://carthagemagazine.com/amazigh-berber-heritage-tunisia/);
   [WildyNess](https://wildyness.com/news/visit-douiret-chenini-berber-village-tunisia)). One of
   the last living Berber-speaking pockets of a country that mostly forgot it was Amazigh.

5. **Testour is a four-century-old Andalusian exile you can still hear.** Rebuilt in the **17th
   century** by **Moriscos** expelled from Spain, **Testour** in the Medjerda valley keeps the
   **malouf**, the Arab-Andalusian music carried across the sea, alive at **Tunisia's oldest
   traditional-music festival**; its Great Mosque carries a **clock whose hands run
   counter-clockwise** — read locally as the exiles' wish to wind time back toward Andalusia
   ([The Arab Weekly](https://thearabweekly.com/testour-tunisias-symbol-religious-coexistence);
   [The National](https://www.thenationalnews.com/news/mena/2024/11/08/postcard-from-testour-how-pomegranates-became-a-symbol-of-tunisian-citys-andalusian-heritage/);
   [Tunisian malouf, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunisian_malouf)).

6. **Gabès holds the Mediterranean's only seaside oasis — and it is being poisoned at its
   edge.** The **oasis of Gabès** runs in three tiers — date palm over pomegranate and henna
   over vegetable — straight down to the shore, watered for millennia; but the state
   **phosphate works (GCT)** have discharged an estimated **tens of millions of tonnes of
   phosphogypsum** into the gulf since the 1970s, and farmers report henna, pomegranate and
   fish failing as salt and pollution rise
   ([Med-O-Med](https://medomed.org/featured_item/gabes-oasis-cultural-landscape-tunisia/);
   [Coda Story](https://www.codastory.com/waronscience/pollution-in-tunisia/);
   [TIMEP](https://timep.org/2022/10/27/gabes-a-victim-of-industrial-pollution-in-tunisia/)).
   A living agricultural landscape and the industry pressing on it — **tension, not indictment;
   the gardeners, not the smokestack, are the subject.**

7. **Black Tunisia's healing liturgy survives in a handful of houses.** In a few houses in
   **Tunis** — above all **Dar Barnou**, once a refuge for freed slaves — the descendants of
   sub-Saharan people brought across the Sahara keep **Stambeli**, a night music of the *gambri*
   lute and iron *shqasheq* that calls a pantheon of sub-Saharan spirits and Muslim saints to
   **heal through trance**. It is documented as surviving in only a few brotherhoods
   ([Stambeli, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stambali); Richard C. Jankowsky,
   *Stambeli: Music, Trance, and Alterity in Tunisia*,
   [U. Chicago Press](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/S/bo9299440.html);
   [Pan-African Music](https://pan-african-music.com/en/stambeli-2-0/)). **Consent-led; ritual,
   not spectacle.**

8. **One of the Mediterranean's last working tuna traps still runs at Sidi Daoud.** At **Sidi
   Daoud**, on the tip of **Cap Bon**, fishermen set the **madrague** — a labyrinth of nets the
   locals call the **matanza** — to trap migrating Atlantic **bluefin** between **May and July**,
   a Phoenician-old technique that ends in a churning chamber of tuna hauled by hand. Quotas,
   industrial fleets and warming water have nearly stilled it
   ([INSTM Bulletin, Sidi Daoud madrague](https://www.instm-bulletin.tn/index.php/bulletin/article/view/713);
   [Mattanza, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mattanza);
   [harissa.com](https://harissa.com/D_Communautes/Tunisie/lapecheauthonasididaoud.htm)).

9. **For two weeks each spring the Cap Bon coast smells of one flower.** Around **Nabeul**,
   families pick **bitter-orange blossom** by hand before dawn in **April–May**, when the scent
   peaks, and distil it over slow fire in copper alembics into **zhar** (orange-blossom water,
   for sweets and grief and celebration) and the precious **neroli** oil — roughly a **million
   flowers** to a kilogram of oil
   ([Africanews](https://www.africanews.com/2022/05/22/nabeul-the-tunisian-capital-of-orange-blossom-water/);
   [Middle East Eye](https://www.middleeasteye.net/discover/tunisia-floral-waters-why-tunisians-love);
   [Biolandes](https://www.biolandes.com/biolandes-livre-neroli.php?voyage=o&lg=en)).

10. **Mahdia weds in gold-threaded silk on a loom that came across the sea.** In the sea-walled
    medina of **Mahdia**, weavers still work tall **draw-looms** — a technology that crossed the
    Mediterranean from Iberia — to make narrow silk strips threaded with gold and silver for the
    **keswa el-kbira**, the layered Sahel wedding dress that signals a woman's married status; the
    craft, brought by Jewish artisans, passed into the town's families
    ([Carthage Magazine](https://carthagemagazine.com/tunisian-wedding/);
    [Discover Tunisia, regional crafts](https://www.discovertunisia.com/en/tunisie-arts-and-crafts/regional-crafts)).

11. **The holy city knots its memory by hand.** **Kairouan** — Islam's fourth holy city and its
    first in Africa, with a UNESCO-listed medina — is also Tunisia's **carpet capital**, where
    women knot the **zerbia** (up to hundreds of thousands of knots a square metre) in workshops
    around the Great Mosque, and bake the date-and-semolina **makroudh** the city claims as its
    own; both loom and oven are pressed by cheaper imports
    ([Discover Tunisia, weaving & carpets](https://www.discovertunisia.com/en/tunisie-arts-and-crafts/weaving-and-carpets);
    [Folk Culture, Kairouan carpet](https://folkculturebh.org/en/?issue=55&page=article&id=1145)).

12. **There is a green, wet, oak-forested Tunisia that contradicts the postcard.** In the
    **Kroumirie** range above **Tabarka**, where it snows in winter, the **Khroumir** mountain
    people harvest **cork** on its long bark-cycle among a cork-oak forest unlike anywhere else
    in the country, herding and gathering in the wettest microclimate in Tunisia
    ([Carthage Magazine, Aïn Draham](https://carthagemagazine.com/ain-draham-travel-guide/);
    [Tunisi.info, Kroumirie](https://www.tunisi.info/en/tour-della-kroumirie-the-forest-of-ain-draham-and-beni-mtir/)).
    The Tunisia of oak and rain, not palm and dune.

**On the bench (researched, held for a later round):** the **Guellala** underground Berber
potteries of Djerba; the **Matmata** troglodyte dwellings; the **Aïssawa / Sufi brotherhoods**
of the zawiyas; the ancient **olive groves of the Sahel** around El Jem; and **couscous** as
ritual infrastructure (UNESCO 2020, shared across the Maghreb). Each is real but either thinner
on a single named custodian or too diffuse to clear the bar this round.

## Where to embed (named places, custodians, seasons, access)

**The South & the Islands:**
- **Kerkennah Islands (Sfax)** — *charfia* fishing families; the palm-fence **setting** is
  seasonal (autumn) and the lift is at dawn. Access via the islands' fishing community and the
  Sfax marine-heritage networks; the practice is UNESCO-documented.
- **Djerba** — Ibadi **mosques** (Houmt Souk hinterland, Guellala, Sedouikech), the **menzel**
  farmsteads, and **El Ghriba** at **Erriadh / Hara Seghira**; the **Lag BaOmer** pilgrimage is
  a fixed spring date. Consent and discretion at the pilgrimage; access via the Djerba Jewish
  community committee and the island's heritage office.
- **Dahar (Tataouine)** — **Chenini** and **Douiret**; the olive-and-barley year and the
  **ghorfa** granaries. Tamazight-speaking elders to be identified with local associations.
- **Gabès** — the **seaside oasis** (Chenini Gabès, Jara) and **henna** growers; the layered
  garden and the old irrigation, against the gulf's industrial edge — handled as tension.

**Cap Bon & the Sahel:**
- **Sidi Daoud (Cap Bon)** — the **madrague / matanza**; the net-rooms are built and the trap
  worked **May–July**. Access via the fishery and El Haouaria.
- **Nabeul / Hammamet (Cap Bon)** — bitter-orange **blossom** picking and **neroli/zhar**
  distillation, **April–May**, family stills (*qattara*).
- **Mahdia** — draw-loom **silk** weavers in the medina; a bride's family for the **keswa**.
- **Kairouan** — **zerbia** carpet-weaving families and a **makroudh** house in the medina.

**The Green North:**
- **Sejnane (Bizerte / Mogods)** — women **potters**; the full clay-to-firing cycle, roadside
  sale. Named makers via the Sejnane women's pottery associations.
- **Testour (Béja / Medjerda)** — **malouf** musician families; the festival (summer) and the
  backward-clock Great Mosque.
- **Tunis** — **Stambeli** brotherhoods at **Dar Barnou / Sidi Saad**; consent-led night
  ceremonies.
- **Aïn Draham / Kroumirie (Jendouba)** — **cork**-harvesting families; the summer stripping
  season and the forest year.

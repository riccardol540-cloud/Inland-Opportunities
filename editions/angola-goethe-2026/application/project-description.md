## Summary (the project in five sentences)

Angola is internationally legible through two stories — the 1975–2002 civil war and the
oil-and-diamond economy that made Luanda one of the world's most expensive cities. Both are
loud; both crowd out the quieter country. *The Fire That Stays Lit* is an equitable
Africa–Europe co-production in which an Angolan cultural partner and a European publication
build a long-form visual record of living Angolan traditions — made **with** their custodians,
not about them. Across every story we ask one question: what persists in specific families,
villages and practices that has been absorbed by neither the colonial inheritance, the
Catholic mission, the long war, nor the petro-modernisation of the coast — and can still be
witnessed and photographed today. The result is a touring exhibition and a printed edition
presented in both Angola and Europe.

## Artistic concept

INLAND's method is slow, place-based and built on trust: months of pre-production research,
then embedding photographers, writers and filmmakers with named custodians over extended field
periods. For Angola the desk research is advanced — **17 leads identified, 12 selected** — each
centred on a **practice that can still be witnessed, a locatable place, and a named custodian
or custodian community** (see the attached research report, selected leads, and Topics &
Contacts spreadsheet). The edition takes its emblem from the **ondjango fire** of the Ovimbundu
council house, kept permanently alight as a sign of lineage and continuity.

The edition foregrounds **endangered knowledge systems and under-documented living practices**
surviving despite rupture, standardisation and generational drift — from the UNESCO-listed but
fragile **sona** sand-writing of the Lunda-Chokwe to the **Kianda** sea-offerings of Luanda's
original fishing people. Two editorial commitments shape every story: the **civil war is
context, never the lens**, and the **state is never cast as villain** — tension without
antagonism.

## Why an Africa–Europe co-production (programme fit)

The work cannot and should not be made by a European team alone. The Angolan partner leads on
community access, consent, language (Portuguese plus Umbundu, Chokwe, Kimbundu and
Oshikwanyama interpretation) and cultural interpretation, and co-authors the selection; INLAND
brings editorial, photographic and publishing capacity and the European presentation. Both
partners share authorship, decision-making and dissemination — the equitable, dialogue-based
co-creation the programme exists to support. The output is presented to audiences in **both
Angola and Europe**, building a durable relationship beyond a single grant.

## The work (three production tracks + a signature story)

A pilot deploys three field clusters, each grouping stories that share geography and season:

1. **The Lunda Cluster (East)** — the *sona* sand-writing masters around Saurimo/Dundo and the
   *mukanda* / *mwana pwo* mask rite. The art heartland; access via Lueji A'Nkonde University
   and the Dundo Museum. (Dry-season camps.)
2. **The Planalto Cluster (Central Highlands)** — the *ondjango* council fire at Bailundo
   (King Ekuikui VI's court); the Amboim robusta coffee revival in Kwanza Sul; highland
   potters and weavers. Governance, return and craft. (Coffee harvest.)
3. **The South & Coast Cluster** — Mwila and Mucubal pastoral knowledge on the Huíla plateau
   and Namibe; the *efundula* female initiation in Cunene (consent-led); salt and artisanal
   fishing at Baía Farta. Body, herd and sea.

A signature long-form story — **the Festa da Ilha and the offerings to Kianda** on the Ilha de
Luanda (the November feast) — runs across the project as its recurring image, paired with the
northern memory leads (the **lumbu** of Mbanza Kongo; **Njinga's footprints** at Pungo Andongo)
pursued as a Kingdoms-and-Memory strand.

## Outputs & public presentation

- A printed INLAND Angola edition (≈170–240 pp), co-credited with the Angolan partner.
- A touring photographic **exhibition** shown in Angola (with the partner) and in Europe.
- A short film / digital story set.
- Custodians receive prints, credit, and a copy of the edition; community presentations in the
  places the work was made.

## Method, ethics & sustainability

Consent-led throughout; custodians are named collaborators, not subjects. Sensitive material
(the *efundula* initiation; the *mukanda* seclusion; the Kianda rite) is handled with explicit,
revocable consent and identity protection where needed, and is never staged for the camera. The
partnership is designed to outlast the grant: a shared archive, joint authorship, and a
presentation in Angola that returns the work to the communities that made it.

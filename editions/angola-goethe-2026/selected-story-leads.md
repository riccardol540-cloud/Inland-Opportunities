# INLAND Angola — Selected Story Leads

*The curated subset chosen for editorial development from the deep-research corpus. Twelve
leads, balanced across INLAND's categories. Each is structured as **Place · Angle · Practices
· Status**, preserving the research trail. For the Goethe Africa–Europe Visual Arts
application, leads marked ★ form the proposed first production clusters; the signature
long-form story is marked ✦.*

---

## 01 ★ The Men Who Write in Sand — *Sona*
**Category:** Arts & Culture / History

- **Place:** Chokwe-Lunda heartland — **Saurimo (Lunda Sul)** and **Dundo (Lunda Norte)**;
  access and documentation via **Lueji A'Nkonde University** (which coordinated the UNESCO
  nomination) and the **Dundo Museum**.
- **Angle:** A continuous-line geometric "writing in sand" — *sona* — that carries proverbs,
  fables and cosmology, learned from masters called **akwa kuta sona**, possibly a pre-colonial
  visual tradition over two thousand years old. Inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural
  Heritage list in **December 2023** — recognised at the exact moment it is most fragile.
- **Practices:** Cleaning and dotting the sand; drawing a single unbroken line by hand or
  lamplight; storytelling and initiation transmission; the mathematics elders never called
  mathematics.
- **Status:** Endangered living tradition; depends on a shrinking number of elder masters.
  The defining image of the edition.
  *Sources: UNESCO ICH 2023 (Ver Angola; Macao News); Lusona (Wikipedia); FLM Journal.*

## 02 ★ The Fire That Stays Lit — the *Ondjango*
**Category:** History / Local communities

- **Place:** **Bailundo, Huambo** — the *ombala* (royal court) of **King Ekuikui VI**, and the
  **ondjango** council houses of the surrounding highlands.
- **Angle:** The Ovimbundu council house, built around a hearth "kept permanently alight,
  symbolising the continuity of the authority of the soba and his lineage of ancestrality."
  Collective speech, metaphoric oratory and consensus — a living African institution of
  governance, not a museum piece. The king today even travels to Brazil's *quilombo*
  communities descended from people taken from these hills.
- **Practices:** The permanently-lit fire; the wood the young bring to feed it; metaphoric
  council speech; the soba's authority and lineage.
- **Status:** Living; a non-sovereign monarchy whose authority is real at the community level.
  *Sources: Bailundo (Wikipedia); UNILAB / NJINGA e SEPÉ; Africanews; Ver Angola.*

## 03 ★ Hair, Herd and Drought — the Mwila and the Mucubal
**Category:** Nature / Local communities

- **Place:** **Huíla plateau** (Lubango, Chibia, Gambos) and **Namibe** — Mwila (Mumuíla) and
  Mucubal/Kuvale pastoral households.
- **Angle:** A culture worn on the body and carried in the herd: the Mwila *nontombi* ochre
  plaits and the Mucubal *ompota* headdress encode life-stage, marriage and mourning, while
  the cattle economy is the base of religious and social identity. Now under drought and
  policy pressure, with the **ethnoveterinary knowledge** of the transhumance routes
  documented as endangered.
- **Practices:** Ochre-and-fibre hairdressing read as a social language; daily and seasonal
  transhumance; herd healing and water-point knowledge.
- **Status:** Living but precarious; climate stress on the south-west rangelands.
  *Sources: Minority Rights Group; kwekudee tripdown; PMC ethnoveterinary study 2024.*

## 04 ✦ Offerings to Kianda — the Festa da Ilha
**Category:** Myth & Religion / Food

- **Place:** **Ilha de Luanda** — the **Axiluanda** ("people of the land of the fishermen"),
  whose name is Luanda's own. The signature recurring scene of the project.
- **Angle:** Every **second Friday of November** the island's fishing families cast food and
  symbolic offerings into the sea to **Kianda** (Dandalunda), the female water spirit who
  protects fishermen — a midnight rite that persists as a luxury-waterfront redevelopment
  pushes the fishers off their own sandbar. Two relationships to the same sea.
- **Practices:** Night offerings to the water; the fishing calendar; gender and the spirit
  (Kianda as protector and mother); the feast.
- **Status:** Living and datable (the November feast); the community is squeezed by coastal
  development. Tension, not antagonism.
  *Sources: Kianda (Wikipedia); Ilha de Luanda (Wikipedia).*

## 05 ★ The Mask That Dances a Woman — *mukanda* and *mwana pwo*
**Category:** Arts & Culture / Myth & Religion

- **Place:** Chokwe country, **Lunda / Moxico**; the **Dundo Museum** for the collected masks,
  the bush camps for the living rite.
- **Angle:** The world's museums are full of Chokwe masks; the rite that animates them is
  barely filmed. During **mukanda**, the months-long boys' circumcision-and-education camp, a
  male dancer becomes **mwana pwo**, an idealised female ancestor — entertaining the mothers
  separated from their sons and teaching Chokwe law, craft and gender. The distance between
  the vitrine and the dust of the camp is the story.
- **Practices:** *Mukanda* seclusion and instruction; the *vilombola* caretakers; carving and
  wearing *mwana pwo*, *cikungu* and dance masks; the dance as social event.
- **Status:** Living, seasonal (dry-season camps), and almost unrecorded as lived practice.
  Consent- and discretion-led around the initiation itself.
  *Sources: Chokwe people (Wikipedia); High Museum / DIA mask scholarship.*

## 06 ★ The Coffee That Went Wild — Amboim robusta
**Category:** Food / History

- **Place:** **Amboim / Gabela (Kwanza Sul)** and **Uíge** — family robusta farms on land the
  war returned to forest.
- **Angle:** Angola was the world's third-largest coffee producer until the war turned the
  plantations into wild bush. Now families harvest from trees their grandparents planted, and
  the EU/AFD-financed **Mukafe** scheme (since 2023) is replanting Amboim — the most valuable
  robusta in the country. A story of return told through one harvest.
- **Practices:** Picking, drying and sorting feral and replanted robusta; the washing-station
  cycle; the memory of the colonial *roça* and what replaced it.
- **Status:** Reviving; ~78% family farming; harvest season (≈May–August) is the visual core.
  Named cooperative/family to confirm in the field.
  *Sources: Perfect Daily Grind; STiR Coffee & Tea; Coffee production in Angola (Wikipedia).*

## 07 The Council That Outlived the King — the *lumbu* of Mbanza Kongo
**Category:** History / Myth & Religion

- **Place:** **Mbanza Kongo (Zaire province)** — capital of the old Kongo kingdom, a **UNESCO
  World Heritage Site (2017)**.
- **Angle:** There has been no Kongo king for decades, yet the **lumbu** — a tribunal of
  **twenty-one elders** that advised the throne for over five hundred years — still meets. An
  institution that outlived the institution it served, in a city where the royal past lies
  literally underfoot.
- **Practices:** The elders' council; oral memory of the kingdom; the sacred topography of the
  buried capital and its trees.
- **Status:** Living council; the wider royal ritual is much reduced. A pure "what survived"
  lead.
  *Sources: UNESCO WHC 2017; Met Museum (Kongo).*

## 08 Njinga's Footprints — Pungo Andongo
**Category:** History / Myth & Religion

- **Place:** **Pedras Negras de Pungo Andongo (Malanje)** — the black monoliths that were the
  Ndongo capital and Queen Njinga's 17th-century stronghold.
- **Angle:** Footprints worn into the rock are venerated locally as those of **Queen Njinga
  (Nzinga Mbande)**, who held off the Portuguese for decades. National memory made physical and
  visited — a shrine without a temple, where a 400-year-old resistance is still touched by hand.
- **Practices:** Local veneration and storytelling at the footprints; the rocks as refuge and
  capital; the landscape as archive.
- **Status:** Living site-memory; informal pilgrimage. Pairs naturally with the Malanje /
  Kalandula circuit.
  *Sources: Black Rocks at Pungo Andongo (Wikipedia); Atlas Obscura.*

## 09 ★ Those Who Ate the Ox — *efundula*
**Category:** Myth & Religion / Local communities

- **Place:** **Cunene** (Kwanyama and Handa areas), into southern Huíla.
- **Angle:** The female initiation rite that marks a girl's passage to womanhood — historically
  including the symbolic "eating of the ox" — surviving in tension with the school and the
  state, as recent research follows young mothers in Cunene caught between the ceremony and
  school dropout. Continuity and its costs, held without judgement.
- **Practices:** The initiation cycle; instruction in adult roles; the community feast; the
  negotiation with formal schooling.
- **Status:** Living and contested; **consent-sensitive** — pursued only with the community,
  identities protected where needed, never staged.
  *Sources: Efundula research (Academia.edu); UCP study, Cunene; ResearchGate (Handa "efuko").*

## 10 ★ Salt and the Long Boats — Baía Farta
**Category:** Food / Nature

- **Place:** **Baía Farta (Benguela)** — historic **salt pans** and the **Casa dos Pescadores
  Artesanais**.
- **Angle:** Two squeezed coastal trades in one bay: solar **salt** evaporated in pans that
  gave the beach its name, and an **artisanal fishing** economy of tens of thousands, both
  pressed by industrial trawling and packaged substitutes. The food infrastructure of the
  coast, made by hand.
- **Practices:** Pan salt-raking and drying; net and line fishing from open boats; drying,
  salting and selling the catch; the knowledge taught at the Casa dos Pescadores.
- **Status:** Living but under economic pressure; a working, photographable calendar.
  *Sources: Journey Gourmet (Benguela); trackstick (Baía Farta); DLIST Benguela.*

## 11 Clay and Fibre — women potters and basket-weavers
**Category:** Arts & Culture / Food

- **Place:** Highland and eastern workshops (**Huambo / Bié / Moxico**) — to be anchored to a
  **named maker**.
- **Angle:** Coil pottery and sisal/palm basketry made by methods passed down generations, now
  half-commercialised for an urban and tourist market — the domestic vessels of Angolan
  food-and-household life, and the women who still make them.
- **Practices:** Hand-coiled and pit-fired pottery; sisal/palm weaving of baskets, mats and
  granaries; the market that is both lifeline and pressure.
- **Status:** Living; commercialising. **Needs a named workshop/maker** in the field to clear
  the bar (currently held just inside the selection on the strength of the practice and place).
  *Sources: African Sahara; Design Milk (Angolan basketry & ceramics).*

## 12 The Most Expensive City and the Musseque
**Category:** Geopolitics / Local communities

- **Place:** **Luanda** — the Marginal's oil-built towers and the self-built *musseques* and
  *kitanda* markets a few hundred metres inland; anchored on the **Ilha de Luanda** fishing
  community (links to lead 04).
- **Angle:** A skyline built on oil stands beside the sandbar where families still fish and
  pray to Kianda. One human — a fisherman, a market woman — carries the frame, so the
  contradiction is portrait, not abstraction.
- **Practices:** Daily fishing and market life against the petro-city; two economies sharing
  one shore.
- **Status:** Living present; **needs a human anchor** to avoid abstraction (the Ilha fishers
  provide it).
  *Sources: Ilha de Luanda (Wikipedia); INLAND field framing.*

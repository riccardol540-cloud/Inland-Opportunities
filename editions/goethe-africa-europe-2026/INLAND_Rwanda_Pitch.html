ABOUT

About INLAND

INLAND is a yearly publication documenting the soul of countries before they change. Each issue embeds with named, locatable custodians of living traditions — artisans, pastoralists, cooks, singers, border families — whose stories have not yet been narrated internationally. Previous issues have covered the Faroe Islands, Georgia and Italy’s minor islands.
The editorial method is slow, place-based and built on trust. We spend months in pre-production research, identify specific custodians and communities, then commission photographers, writers and filmmakers to embed with them over extended field periods. The result is long-form visual storytelling grounded in named people and real places.
Intimacy with custodians, not thematic surveys.
THE RESEARCH

Rwanda — What Survived

Rwanda is internationally legible through two stories — the 1994 genocide and the development ‘miracle’. Both are loud; both crowd out the quieter country. INLAND asks one question across every lead: what survived? What persists in specific families, villages and practices that has been absorbed by neither the colonial inheritance, the Christian mission, nor the modernisation project — and can still be witnessed and photographed today.
Our first round of research identified 20 story leads spanning communities, ritual, food, ecology, craft and music. From these, twelve storylines have been selected for editorial development — each centred on a named custodian, a specific place, and a practice that can still be witnessed.

EDITORIAL ANGLE

Our Angle

We focus on endangered knowledge systems and under-documented living practices surviving despite standardisation, policy change and generational drift. The genocide is context, never the lens; the state is never cast as villain. Every storyline centres a named custodian, a specific place, and a practice that can still be witnessed.
This is the equitable, co-created Africa–Europe cultural work the Goethe programme exists to fund: a Rwandan cultural partner and a European publication building a long-form visual record of living Rwandan traditions — made with custodians, not about them, and presented in both Rwanda and Europe.

What They Carry

Batwa Forest Knowledge
The Story
Batwa elders around Musanze hold centuries-deep ecological knowledge of the Nyungwe and Virunga forests — plant identification, animal behaviour, spatial memory — now sought by conservation biologists before it disappears. ~25,000–30,000 Batwa, officially ‘historically marginalised’.
Our Proposal
INLAND embeds with elders on a plant walk and records the forest songs — documenting the knowledge itself rather than the (already filmed) displacement. The narrative asks what becomes of forest knowledge when there is no forest left to use it in.
Places: Musanze · Kibingo · forest margins
“Not what was taken — what they still carry.”
Sources: AIMPO; INLAND Rwanda research corpus

Myth & Religion

The Rites That Won’t Die

Kubandwa / Ryangombe
The Story
A pre-colonial spirit-possession system centred on Ryangombe that missionaries tried to erase, surviving underground in a 92.8% Christian country. A 2021 academic recorded students attending rites ‘even in the centre of Kigali’.
Our Proposal
INLAND follows the practice through discreet, consent-led fieldwork and academic introductions — documenting an adaptive ritual that crosses linguistic, political and temporal frontiers. If confirmed in the field, the edition’s signature story.
Places: Kigali · discreet rural sites
“Gone underground, not extinct.”
Sources: Pennacini (Turin); Bizimana, RCSP Rwanda 2018

Myth & Religion

The Hospital Sends You to a Healer

Uburozi and Traditional Medicine
The Story
In Kinigi, Musanze — ‘the core location for healers’ — hospitals refer patients to traditional healers for uburozi, a sorcery/poisoning illness conventional medicine doesn’t recognise. ~5,000 healers nationally; a 2022 Traditional Medicine policy exists.
Our Proposal
INLAND shadows a referral from clinic to healer and back, with portraits of both — documenting a state-acknowledged parallel healthcare system operating in the open and almost entirely unreported internationally.
Places: Kinigi · Burera · Bushozi · Musenyi
“A parallel medicine, acknowledged and unseen.”
Sources: BMC Compl. Med. & Therapies 2021; Rwanda FDA 2022

04

Crushed by Foot

Urwagwa, the Banana Beer of Rites
The Story
Banana beer is ritual infrastructure — the medium of marriage negotiations, ancestral offerings and reconciliation. Named brewer Agatha Tuyisenge (Mushubati, Rutsiro), mother of five, built a livelihood from inherited brewing knowledge.
Our Proposal
INLAND follows one full brewing cycle toward a wedding — variety selection, foot-crushing, sorghum fermentation — and the ceremonial economy around the beer. A named protagonist with a full story arc.
Places: Mushubati · Rutsiro District
“Not a drink — the medium ceremonies happen in.”
Sources: SENS Magazine (Tuyisenge profile)

The Last Sorghum Beer

Ikigage on the Volcano Slopes
The Story
A sorghum beer of feasts, dowries and harvests near Kinigi and Musanze, now on the Slow Food Ark of Taste — declining as sorghum is squeezed out by the Crop Intensification Programme.
Our Proposal
INLAND documents a household that still grows sorghum and brews ikigage for weddings — a compressed portrait of what agricultural policy does to cultural memory.
Places: Kinigi · Musanze
“A beer on the Ark of Taste, brewed for one more wedding.”
Sources: Slow Food Ark of Taste; Slow Food Rwanda

06
Myth & Religion

A Harvest Without Its Secrets

Umuganura and the Abiru
The Story
Umuganura was a sacred rite of ubwiru guarded by the hereditary Abiru, needing sacred sorghum from Huro (Gakenke). Abolished by the Belgians in 1925; revived in 2011 as a secular holiday — without the Abiru, the plants, or the secret language.
Our Proposal
INLAND shoots the intact-but-vacant ritual site at Huro against the August state festival — a ceremony hollowed of meaning and repurposed for nation-building. A story about what revival leaves out.
Places: Huro · Bumbogo · Gakenke District
“The site survived. The secret did not.”
Sources: ubwiru / Abiru historiography

07
Food

The Morality of Milk

Kivuguto and the Inkongoro Gourd
The Story
Fermented milk made in handmade inkongoro gourds carrying unique indigenous microbial cultures — the moral order encoded in how milk is stored, fermented and shared, now replaced by steel tanks and controlled starters.
Our Proposal
INLAND builds a diptych: morning milking and gourd fermentation in a pastoral household, against a modern dairy plant making packaged ‘yogurt drinks’. Elders speak to the symbolism of the gourd.
Places: Pastoral households · Musanze / Gishwati
“A microbial culture is also a moral one.”
Sources: INLAND Rwanda research corpus

Local communities

Life After the Forest

The Gishwati Resettlement
The Story
Families moved off the Gishwati–Mukura forests now farm ~0.5 ha on steep, flood-prone slopes in Nyabihu, Rutsiro and Rubavu. Some, like Laurent Hategekimana, patrol as unpaid auxiliaries the forest margins their families were removed from.
Our Proposal
INLAND walks the forest margin with a patroller and spends time in the resettlement villages — the long, quiet afterlife of displacement on a landscape remade by ranching, floods and restoration.
Places: Nyabihu · Rutsiro · Rubavu · Bigogwe
“Guarding the forest that displaced them.”
Sources: Rainforest Journalism Fund; Pulitzer Center

Food

Thirty Years On

The Reconciliation Coffee Cooperatives
The Story
Cooperatives built post-1994 to put Hutu and Tutsi farmers in the same washing stations — Maraba (Huye) and Dukunde Kawa Musasa (Gakenke). The origin story is told; the present is unfilmed.
Our Proposal
INLAND follows one or two families through a harvest — picking, sorting, washing — asking quietly what changed in a generation and what didn’t. The founders are middle-aged now; their children work side by side.
Places: Maraba (Huye) · Dukunde Kawa (Gakenke)
“The origin story is told. The present is not.”
Sources: Alliance for Coffee Excellence; cooperative profiles

Arts & Culture

The Last Maestro

Vianey Mushabizi and the Inanga
The Story
A single instrument and the man who may be the end of its line: Vianey Mushabizi (Gatagara), possibly the last living master of the inanga zither — the sound of a pre-radio Rwanda held in one pair of hands.
Our Proposal
INLAND records his repertoire and tuning, and asks the question of transmission: is there an apprentice, or does the line end here? A portrait in sound and image.
Places: Gatagara
“A whole tradition in one pair of hands.”
Sources: INLAND Rwanda research corpus

Arts & Culture

Geometry of the Widows

Imigongo and the Market
The Story
Geometric cow-dung relief paintings made by genocide widows in Kakira, Eastern Province — now commercialised. A tension between cultural preservation, livelihood and market co-option.
Our Proposal
INLAND documents the making and the market in one place — who paints, who sells, who profits when a domestic craft scales into a souvenir.
Places: Kakira · Eastern Province
“Who profits when a tradition scales?”
Sources: INLAND Rwanda research corpus

The Data Centre and the Hill

Two Relationships to Electricity
The Story
A windowless server warehouse runs 24 hours at full load a few kilometres from villages where electricity arrived three years ago. The guard at the gate is from such a village.
Our Proposal
INLAND anchors the development showcase in one human — the guard — to portray two relationships to electricity, land and the future coexisting within a few kilometres, without abstraction.
Places: Eastern Province / peri-Kigali
“Two centuries, a few kilometres apart.”
Sources: INLAND Rwanda research corpus

Season: Coffee harvest; August (Umuganura)
2–3 stories, seasonal windows
THE PARTNERSHIP

An Equitable Co-Production

European partner
INLAND — yearly publication (Italy / EU). Editorial, photography and publishing; presentation of the work in Europe.
African partner
A Rwandan cultural organisation (lead candidate: Red Rocks Initiatives, Musanze) — co-creation, community access, custodian liaison, presentation in Rwanda.
Funding path
Mobility grant (≤€4,000) funds the scouting trip; the Visual Arts grant funds collaborative production and a public presentation in Rwanda and Europe.
The principle
Made with custodians, not about them — equitable Africa–Europe co-creation, exactly the dialogue the Goethe programme exists to fund.

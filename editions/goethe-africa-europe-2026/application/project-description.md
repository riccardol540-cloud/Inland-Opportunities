## Summary (the project in five sentences)

Rwanda is internationally legible through two stories — the 1994 genocide and the
development "miracle." Both are loud; both crowd out the quieter country. *What Survived*
is an equitable Africa–Europe co-production in which a Rwandan cultural partner and a
European publication build a long-form visual record of living Rwandan traditions — made
**with** their custodians, not about them. Across every story we ask one question: what
persists in specific families, villages and practices that has been absorbed by neither the
colonial inheritance, the Christian mission, nor the modernisation project — and can still
be witnessed and photographed today. The result is a touring exhibition and a printed
edition presented in both Rwanda and Europe.

## Artistic concept

INLAND's method is slow, place-based and built on trust: months of pre-production research,
then embedding photographers, writers and filmmakers with named custodians over extended
field periods. For Rwanda the research is already advanced — 20 leads identified, 12
selected — each centred on a **named custodian, a specific place, and a practice that can
still be witnessed** (see the attached research report, selected leads, and Topics &
Contacts spreadsheet).

The edition foregrounds **endangered knowledge systems and under-documented living
practices** surviving despite standardisation, policy change and generational drift. Two
editorial commitments shape every story: the **genocide is context, never the lens**, and
the **state is never cast as villain** — tension without antagonism.

## Why an Africa–Europe co-production (programme fit)

The work cannot and should not be made by a European team alone. The Rwandan partner leads
on community access, consent, language and cultural interpretation and co-authors the
selection; INLAND brings editorial, photographic and publishing capacity and the European
presentation. Both partners share authorship, decision-making and dissemination — the
equitable, dialogue-based co-creation the programme exists to support. The output is
presented to audiences in **both** Rwanda and Europe, building a durable relationship
beyond a single grant.

## The work (three production tracks)

A pilot deploys three field clusters, each grouping stories that share geography and timing:

1. **The Musanze Cluster** — Batwa forest knowledge; the hospital-to-healer *uburozi*
   referral in Kinigi; *ikigage* sorghum beer. One northern base, three custodian-led
   stories.
2. **Brewing & Land** — the *urwagwa* banana-beer cycle in Rutsiro (brewer Agatha
   Tuyisenge); the Gishwati resettlement in Nyabihu (Laurent Hategekimana); the *kivuguto*
   milk diptych. Ritual food and the afterlife of displacement.
3. **Coffee & Memory** — the reconciliation coffee cooperatives thirty years on
   (Maraba, Dukunde Kawa); the hollowed *Umuganura* harvest at Huro; the *imigongo*
   widows. Memory, harvest and craft.

A signature long-form story — *Kubandwa / Ryangombe*, the pre-colonial rite surviving
underground — runs across the project, pursued only with discretion and consent and **only
if confirmed in the field.**

## Outputs & public presentation

- A printed INLAND Rwanda edition (≈170–240 pp), co-credited with the Rwandan partner.
- A touring photographic **exhibition** shown in Rwanda (with the partner) and in Europe.
- A short film / digital story set.
- Custodians receive prints, credit, and a copy of the edition; community presentations in
  the places the work was made.

## Method, ethics & sustainability

Consent-led throughout; custodians are named collaborators, not subjects. Sensitive
material (Kubandwa; the *uburozi* healers; the Haguruka legal-aid cases) is handled with
explicit, revocable consent and identity protection where needed. The partnership is
designed to outlast the grant: a shared archive, joint authorship, and a presentation in
Rwanda that returns the work to the communities that made it.

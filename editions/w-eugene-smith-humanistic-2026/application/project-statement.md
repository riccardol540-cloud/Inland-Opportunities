## Project description  *(field limit: 2,000 characters)*

INLAND is a yearly publication that documents the soul of a country before it becomes
history. Our second issue (Georgia, 2024) embedded with named custodians of living
traditions across the country. "The Quiet Country" is the proposed continuation: a
long-form, humanistic essay about the people who keep Georgia's endangered knowledge alive
in its high valleys and a few plains communities.

Georgia is known internationally through loud stories — the 2008 war and the occupied
lines, the recurring Tbilisi protests, the wine-and-feast brand. All three crowd out the
quieter country. This project asks one question of every life it photographs: what survived,
and can still be witnessed today?

It follows a small cast of named protagonists across a working year: a Tusheti shepherd
family driving the flock over the 2,850-metre Abano Pass; the tower-keeping Chelidzes of
Ushguli; the shrine-priests of Khevsureti at the summer Atengenoba; the Kist women who
alone in the South Caucasus lead the Sufi zikr at Duisi; a Kakhetian family making wine in
clay buried under the floor at harvest; and the last worshippers of a 26-century Jewish
community. The work is made with these custodians, not about them — continuity over crisis,
dignity over spectacle, in the tradition of W. Eugene Smith's concerned photography of the
human condition.

## Project proposal  *(field limit: 3,000 characters)*

"The Quiet Country" extends a body of work I began for INLAND's Georgia edition into a
single, sustained humanistic essay. The method is slow and place-based: months of
pre-production research (a 16-lead study is complete), then extended field embedding with a
small number of named people, returning across seasons so the camera is present for the
real moment, not a staged one.

The essay is organised as a journey, not a survey, around three field clusters that share
geography and calendar. The Eastern Highlands carry the spine: the transhumance over the
Abano Pass in late spring and autumn, Tushetian guda cheese born in a sheepskin on the
summer mountain, the pre-Christian shrine cult of Khevsureti at Atengenoba (100 days after
Easter), and the Friday women's zikr at Duisi in Pankisi — the only place in the region
where women lead the rite, now fading as its elders pass. The Western Voices follow the
tower families and animist sun-hymns of Svaneti, the endangered Svan language sung by the
young, the Gurian krimanchuli, and a Jewish community of 2,600 years down to its last
minyan in Oni and Kutaisi. The Plain and the Desert close on the wine made under the floor
of the house at harvest, the minankari enamel revival in Tbilisi, and the cave monastery of
David Gareja on the disputed desert border.

The recurring image is the Abano Pass crossing; the binding social thread is the supra and
its polyphony, which gathers nearly every protagonist around one table.

Why now: these are not ruins but living practices held by ageing custodians — the last
women of the Duisi zikr, a thinning Svan-speaking generation, a synagogue built for a
thousand and kept by twenty. They are photographable on a knowable calendar, and a few will
not survive the decade.

Ethics are central. Every named protagonist is re-contacted and consents before publication;
sacred and semi-private practice (the shrine cult, the zikr, the Sabbath) is approached as
ritual, not spectacle, with identities protected where asked. The loud geopolitics stays as
context, never the lens; no institution is cast as villain. The output is a cohesive edit of
up to 40 photographs, supporting a printed INLAND edition (~170–240 pp) and digital stories.
The Smith Grant would underwrite the extended field embedding — the returns across seasons —
that this kind of slow, dignified witness requires.

## Extended narrative *(internal — not for the form)*

INLAND's editorial spine is intimacy with custodians whose lives have not been told
internationally. Georgia rewards that method better than almost anywhere: a small country
holding an improbable density of living, pre-modern practice — sun-hymns that name a pagan
god behind Christian words, a wine technology 8,000 years old, a faith of horned stone
shrines and elected priests, a women-led Sufi rite found nowhere else in the South Caucasus.

The Smith tradition is the right frame because Smith's subject was always the dignity of
ordinary life under pressure — the midwife, the country doctor, the people of Minamata. Our
protagonists live that exact register: a shepherd on a lethal pass, a family keeping a
medieval tower warm, women singing a rite toward its own silence. The grant's compassion-
and-craft criteria map onto INLAND's discipline of continuity over crisis.

Practical notes for reviewers: the strongest seasonal windows are late May (transhumance up),
mid-July (Atengenoba), and late September (rtveli harvest and the autumn descent), with
year-round work possible on the choirs, the enamel and the synagogues. See `budget` and
`timeline` for the field plan, and `proposed-project-image-edit.md` for the 40-frame edit.

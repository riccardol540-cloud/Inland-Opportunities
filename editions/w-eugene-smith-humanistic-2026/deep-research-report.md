# INLAND Georgia — Deep-Research Report

*For the W. Eugene Smith Grant in Humanistic Photography 2026. The territory anchor is
**Georgia** — the country of INLAND Issue 02 (2024). This report maps the living
traditions and named custodians for a proposed long-form humanistic essay that extends
INLAND's Georgia body of work into a single, concerned-but-dignified project in the
tradition of W. Eugene Smith.*

---

## TL;DR

Georgia is internationally legible through three loud stories — the 2008 war and the
Russian-occupied lines of Abkhazia and South Ossetia; the recurring Tbilisi street
protests over the country's European future; and the wine-and-feast tourism brand. All
three are loud; all three crowd out the quieter country. INLAND goes to the quieter
country and asks one question across every lead: **what survived — and can still be
witnessed and photographed today?** In Georgia the answer lives in the high valleys of
the Greater Caucasus and in a handful of plains communities: a mounted spring
transhumance older than the nation; defence-tower families who still keep cattle below a
medieval keep; a pre-Christian mountain shrine cult with elected priests; the only place
in the South Caucasus where women lead the Sufi *zikr*; an 8,000-year-old wine made under
the floor of the house; and one of the world's oldest Jewish diasporas thinning toward a
last minyan. This is **concerned photography about the human condition** — Smith's exact
register — and it is photographable on a knowable calendar.

We researched **16 leads**; **12 are selected** for the proposed project, balanced across
Arts & Culture, Food, Myth & Religion, Local communities, History, Nature and Geopolitics.

---

## Editorial frame (carried into every lead)

- **The loud story is context, never the lens.** The occupation lines, the protests and
  the geopolitics of Russia–EU may sit in the background of a frame, but they are never
  the primary subject. INLAND's value is everything the wire services do not photograph.
- **No adversarial framing of the state.** Tension and contradiction are held without
  casting a government as villain — important where a ministry or museum may be a partner.
  *Tension, not attack.*
- **Consent and discretion for living, semi-private practice.** The mountain shrine cult,
  the Pankisi *zikr*, the synagogue: approached with consent, as ritual rather than
  spectacle; named protagonists drawn from secondary reporting are re-contacted and must
  agree before appearing in a public pitch.
- **Voice:** slow, intimate, restrained, cinematic — dignity over spectacle, continuity
  over crisis, the particular over the postcard.

## Key findings (numbered)

### 1. The mounted transhumance is the country's oldest moving image
Around a tenth of Tusheti's people still live to the rhythm of transhumance, driving
sheep (and, increasingly rarely, riding the migration on horseback) between the winter
pastures of the Kakheti and Shida Kartli lowlands and the high summer grass of Tusheti.
The herds climb in late spring and descend in autumn over the **Abano Pass (2,850 m)** —
one of the most dangerous roads in the country — eating alpine bluegrass and toothed
bellflower between June and September. National Geographic documented a 2019 crossing with
a named young shepherd, **Giorgi Karsamauli (29)**; RFE/RL profiled a woman keeping the
*mounted* horse-migration alive when most have switched to trucks. This is the signature
recurring image of the project: a half-millennium-old movement of animals and people,
still crossing a real pass on a real date.
*Sources: Atlas Obscura (transhumance in Tusheti); National Geographic (Caucasus
migration); RFE/RL (the woman keeping the horse migration alive); Milk Trekker.*

### 2. The tower families of Upper Svaneti still keep house beneath the keep
Ushguli — a cluster of villages at the confluence of the Inguri and Black rivers, among
the highest continuously inhabited settlements in Europe — is a UNESCO World Heritage site
of more than 200 medieval *koshki* defence towers. The towers were at once home, fortress
and treasury. A handful of families still live the old way among them: husband and wife
**Roland and Gulo Chelidze** keep cattle, pigs, goats, horses and chickens and look after
the family's defence tower. Horses remain reliable transport across the largely roadless
high country. The frame: one family's winter day between the byre and the tower.
*Sources: UNESCO WHC list 709 (Upper Svaneti); National Geographic (Svanetia); Atlas
Obscura (Towers of Svaneti); Museum of Wander (Ushguli).*

### 3. The Sun-god still sings in the Svan hymns
Svan polyphony is among the oldest layers of Georgian song. Hymns such as **Lile** (the
ancient Svan word for the **Sun God**) and **Kviria** carry largely pre-Christian,
animist texts that Svans today often re-read through a Christian lens. The **Riho
Ensemble** — the Mestia regional folk ensemble — kept these songs for over fifty years
under **Islam (Eptime) Pilpani**, named a "Benefactor of Georgian Folk Song"; his son
**Vakhtang Pilpani** now directs the choir he joined as a teenager. The frame: a rehearsal
in Mestia, three male voices braided into a sun-hymn older than the church.
*Sources: Georgian Chant (Svanetian chant; Kviria); Chanting.ge (Svan folk songs);
Discogs (Riho — Vocal Polyphonies from Svaneti); Tsutisopeli.*

### 4. The highest voice in Europe lives in Guria
On the Black Sea side, Gurian polyphony is the most acrobatic of all Georgian song: above
the texture flies the **krimanchuli**, a high yodel-falsetto that flashes tension and
release. Georgian polyphony was proclaimed by UNESCO in 2001 and inscribed in 2008. In
**Lanchkhuti (Guria)**, the **Turkia family** has been central to preserving and teaching
the Gurian repertoire. The frame: a *supra* table in Guria where a three-part song breaks
into the krimanchuli — the voice as a household inheritance.
*Sources: UNESCO ICH (Georgian polyphonic singing 00008); Georgia/Chortownia (Georgian
polyphony); harmonychroniclesmag.com.*

### 5. The wine is made under the floor of the house
Georgia's claim to 8,000 years of winemaking is lived, not marketed, in the **marani** —
the cellar where egg-shaped **qvevri** are buried to the neck and the wine ferments on its
skins through the cold without machinery. UNESCO inscribed the qvevri method in 2013; PGI
status followed in 2021. The qvevri themselves are still thrown by a few specialist
families — in **Vardisubani (Kakheti)** and Shrosha/Makatubani (Imereti) — and the
knowledge passes through families at the **rtveli** (harvest, Sept–Oct), when whole
households press, ferment and feast together. The frame: a Kakhetian family sealing the
qvevri at rtveli; the potter at Vardisubani firing a vessel taller than a man.
*Sources: UNESCO ICH (qvevri 00870); National Wine Agency (wine.gov.ge); Decanter
(Georgian orange wine); winesgeorgia.com.*

### 6. The mountains keep a faith older than the church
In Khevsureti the people worship at *jvari* / *khati* / *salotsavi* — stone shrines about
two metres tall, hung with **ibex horns** and a small bell — in a syncretism where, behind
the Christian names and candles, the rite is essentially pre-Christian. The shrine is
served by an elected **khevisberi/khutsesi** (shrine-priest), a **dasturi** (servant who
tends the shrine lands and prepares the feast), and, in Pirikita Khevsureti, a
**khatis diasakhlisi** ("hostess of the shrine," a post-menopausal woman of the
community). The great festival, **Atengenoba**, falls 100 days after Easter (high summer),
with beer brewed at the shrine and animals offered. Access via the fortress villages of
**Shatili and Mutso**. The frame: the khevisberi at the horned shrine on Atengenoba.
*Sources: Wikipedia (Khevsureti); academia.edu ("Khatis Diasakhlisi"); Pshav-Khevsureti
sacred places (khconsult); georgia-tours.eu (Khevsureti).*

### 7. The only women's *zikr* in the South Caucasus is fading in Pankisi
The **Kist** of the Pankisi Gorge are descendants of Chechens and Ingush who crossed the
mountains in the mid-1800s; most follow the pacifist Sufi path of **Kunta-Hajji**.
Pankisi is **the only place in the South Caucasus where women perform the *zikr* in the
mosque as men do**: every Friday, Kist women gather at the **Old Mosque in Duisi** to
circle and chant *nazms* in the Kist dialect, calling "marshua kavkaz" — peace in the
Caucasus. The all-female **Aznash Laaman** ensemble carries the chants abroad. The rite is
led by elderly women and, with younger women turning to a more conservative Sunni
practice, is edging toward silence. The frame (consent-led): the Friday circle in Duisi.
*Sources: OC Media (the last women performing zikr in Pankisi); Al Jazeera America (female
Sufi mystics of Pankisi); pankisivalley.com (heritage & culture).*

### 8. The cheese is born in a sheepskin on the summer mountain
**Tushetian guda** is made by herders during the summer transhumance from whole raw cow's
and sheep's milk, matured inside a **guda** (a whole sheepskin sack) and covered with
**nabadi** (the felted Caucasian *burka*) so it takes the salt evenly. The women run the
household and the wool: felt carpets (*pardagi*), the nabadi cloak, headwear. The EU-backed
cooperative **Alaznistavi** has revived guda production in Tusheti, bringing women into the
dairy. The frame: a summer dairy hut, milk into skin, the nabadi over the curing room.
*Sources: EU4Georgia (Alaznistavi guda cooperative); Milk Trekker (Tusheti / guda);
Wikipedia (Tushetians); nlinemedia (Tushetian flavours).*

### 9. One of the world's oldest diasporas is down to its last worshippers
Jews have lived in Georgia for some **26 centuries** — by tradition since the fall of the
First Temple — in what is often called the one country where they were never persecuted as
Jews. That long presence is now very thin. The **Oni Synagogue** (1895, Moorish Revival),
the oldest functioning synagogue in Georgia, once held a congregation of over a thousand
in the Racha mountains; today fewer than 20 regular worshippers remain. Kutaisi's Jewish
quarter on **Boris Gaponov Street** once numbered 30,000 and now counts fewer than a
hundred families across its 19th-century synagogues. The frame: a Sabbath where the minyan
is barely made, in a synagogue built for a thousand.
*Sources: Wikipedia (Oni Synagogue; Kutaisi Synagogue); Gil Travel (2,600 years of Jewish
history in Georgia); jguideeurope.org (Oni).*

### 10. A monastery hewn into a contested desert
**David Gareja** is a complex of 19 medieval cave monasteries — some 5,000 cells cut into
the rock of the Kakheti semi-desert in the 6th century by St David, one of the Thirteen
Assyrian Fathers. The **Udabno** caves hold 8th–13th-century frescoes including a rare
portrait of **Queen Tamar**. The Soviet-drawn **Georgia–Azerbaijan border runs straight
through the complex**, making part of it technically Azerbaijani and the line a live, quiet
dispute; as of 2026 the lower Lavra is open while upper Udabno remains hard to reach. The
frame: a monk on the desert ridge where the frontier and the frescoes meet.
*Sources: UNESCO tentative list 5224 (David Gareji); Advantour; Atlas Obscura (Davit
Gareja); wander-lush.org (2026 access).*

### 11. The fire returns to the enamel
**Minankari**, Georgian cloisonné enamel, reached its height between the 10th and 15th
centuries on icons and crosses, then was effectively abandoned. Over the last two decades
Tbilisi artists have revived it — making works by an almost unchanged hand process. The
**"Ornament"** gallery (founded 2001) is the dedicated centre of the revival, and masters
such as **Thea Gurgenidze** have built names teaching and working the craft. The frame: a
master laying gold wire and firing colour in a Tbilisi workshop — a medieval church
technique alive in living hands.
*Sources: NTD (cloisonné enamel revived in Georgia); mygeotrip (Minankari; Ornament
gallery); georgia.to (enamel art studios).*

### 12. A mountain language dies while its songs come back
Svan — an unwritten Kartvelian language perhaps three millennia divergent from Georgian —
is classed as endangered, spoken by a shrinking number of elders even as Svan *music*
enjoys a youth-led revival driven from **Mestia** (the dance-and-song ensemble **Lileo**,
and young singers learning the old hymns). The paradox is the story: the songs return to
young mouths while the everyday language thins. The frame: a Mestia rehearsal where
teenagers sing words in a tongue fewer of them now speak at home.
*Sources: Chai Khana (Svans, gatekeepers of the mountains); georgianchant.org (Svaneti
revival); UNESCO Atlas of endangered languages (Svan).*

## Where to embed (clusters, geography, season)

**A — The Eastern Highlands (Kakheti gateway).** Tusheti transhumance + guda cheese +
Khevsureti shrine cult + Pankisi *zikr*. Base in Kakheti (Telavi/Akhmeta); up the Abano
Pass to Tusheti (Omalo) and the Datvisjvari road to Khevsureti (Shatili/Mutso); Pankisi
(Duisi) is an hour from Akhmeta. **Season:** transhumance late May & September; guda June–
Sept; **Atengenoba** ~100 days after Easter (mid-July 2026); Pankisi *zikr* any Friday.

**B — The Western Voices.** Svaneti towers + Lile/Riho + Svan language/youth (Mestia,
Ushguli); Gurian krimanchuli (Lanchkhuti, Guria); Georgian Jews (Oni, Racha; Kutaisi).
**Season:** Svaneti accessible late spring–autumn; choir rehearsals year-round; synagogue
Sabbaths year-round (confirm festival dates).

**C — The Plain & the Desert.** Qvevri wine + marani at **rtveli** (Vardisubani/Telavi,
Sept–Oct); David Gareja (Udabno, spring or autumn light); minankari workshop (Tbilisi,
year-round). **Season:** anchor on rtveli (late September).

**Signature image** for the Smith edit: the **Abano Pass crossing** (lead 1) as the
recurring spine; the **supra and polyphony** as the binding social thread across clusters.

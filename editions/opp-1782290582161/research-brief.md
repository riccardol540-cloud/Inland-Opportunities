## Why this fits INLAND

CANTEMIR funds cultural projects that promote Romanian culture in the international environment, specifically in visual arts (photography, new media, design) and performing arts. Events/publications must be produced and distributed **outside Romania** — which is exactly what INLAND does: an international print magazine sold at €30/issue through stockists across Europe and beyond.

An INLAND Romania edition, produced in Italy and distributed internationally, qualifies directly as "a cultural initiative in the visual arts promoting Romanian culture abroad." The 2026 call introduced a unified, multidisciplinary approach encouraging projects that fuse artistic expression — a register INLAND already inhabits.

## Romania as INLAND territory — what survives

Romania's loud story is communist collectivisation, Ceaușescu, and post-1989 transition. INLAND goes elsewhere. The quiet country holds:

**1. Maramureș — living wooden church culture and folk dress**  
The northernmost county, bordering Ukraine, preserves eight UNESCO-listed wooden churches. But the real story is the surrounding villages: families who still commission carpenters to build in the old technique; women who weave the traditional linen ie blouse for their daughters' trousseaux. The craft is alive enough to photograph — a real season (spring weaving, winter church festivals) exists.

**2. Danube Delta — fishing communities under ecological pressure**  
The Delta's Lipoveni (Lipovan Old Believers) have maintained distinct fishing traditions, language (a Russian dialect), and religious practice since the 17th century. Climate change and regulation are squeezing traditional netting and fish species. Named families, locatable villages (Sfântu Gheorghe, Mila 23), a photographable season (spring flood, autumn migration catch).

**3. Transylvanian Saxon villages — a culture with one generation left**  
The Saxons who did not emigrate in 1989 are elderly and few. Their fortified churches, agricultural calendars, and German dialect sit in functioning villages — Viscri, Biertan, Saschiz — where the last custodians can still be named and visited. A story about transmission and absence as much as survival.

**4. Transhumance — shared ICH, Romania's pastoral axis**  
Romania is one of 10 countries on UNESCO's 2023 transhumance ICH inscription. Shepherds from Mărginimea Sibiului still drive flocks south each autumn. Named families, a specific seasonal scene (October drive through mountain passes), direct link to a live UNESCO credential that strengthens the grant framing.

**5. Lăutar / cobzar musical tradition — endangered instrumental lineage**  
The professional musician caste (lăutari, traditionally Roma) who kept Romanian folk music alive for centuries is in sharp contraction. The cobza (short-necked lute) has fewer than a handful of active players. Families who have held the tradition for generations are documentable; concerts at weddings and religious feasts remain real visual scenes.

## Application fit and framing

CANTEMIR requires an applicant based in an EU country (INLAND / Italy qualifies) and favours projects that involve a **Romanian cultural institution as co-partner** (museum, cultural centre, regional foundation). Recommended:

- **Frame**: Romania edition as a photography and editorial project produced abroad (the magazine itself), with field research embedded with Romanian cultural institutions (e.g. Muzeul Etnografic al Transilvaniei, Cluj-Napoca; Complexul Muzeal Bucovina for the north).
- **Required docs (historical pattern)**: Project description, budget, timeline, partner letter from a Romanian institution, proof of prior international cultural activity (INLAND's three published issues serve as track record).
- **Budget fit**: ~€20,000 covers editorial production, 2–3 week field trip, and partial printing costs.
